Understanding Coercion Resistance (Informally) (WIP)

(This is partially a summary of the opening of section 9.4 of Clark's thesis Democracy Enhancing Technologies: Towards Deployable and Incoercible E2E Elections.)


Introduction

Suppose a voter $v$ has been selected for coercion. The voter has intention $I_v$ (it doesn't matter what, whether it's to abstain from voting, vote for a specific candidate, destroy their ballot, and so on).

We will be defining coercion resistance through a Shoup-style game with coin flips. If we get heads, the voter completely complies with the coercer. "More formally, if the voter is an interactive Turing machine (ITM), the coercer learns her internal state and she only forwards messages to and from the coercer." The strategy of purely obeying is called a "dummy strategy".

If the coin is tails, the voter will do her best to "deceive the coercer". Notice that the voter is specifically trying to deceive the coercer, and not ignoring them or something else. "In this case, the voter will try to give the coercer fake information and when she is instructed to do something, she will act as if she did it while not necessarily complying. More formally she only simulates the dummy strategy and may send and receive messages without forwarding them to the coercer."

Now, suppose this game is being played with a trusted party who is responsible for producing the tally. Now, the trusted party flips a coin and if heads uses the coercer's vote and if tails uses $I_v$. This is basically the same coin flip as before, just framed differently.

We say the game (played within an actual system) is coercion-resistant with respect to $I_v$ if the following properties hold:

  1. "if the flip is tails, the voter can always accomplish $I_v$"
  2. "the coercer cannot guess whtether the voter is complying or deceiving him with more (or less) success than he could if he played the same game with the ideal system".
